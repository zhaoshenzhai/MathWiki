Abelian Group


Here, we consider a class of groups which $-$ despite being very general $-$ exhibit very useful properties absent in general groups.

Definition. A group $G$ is said to be Abelian if $gh=hg$ for all $g,h\in G$.

We denote the subcategory of Abelian groups as $\catabgrp$, which is one of the most well-behaved categories. Indeed, ???.

  • We have a forgetful functor $\catmod[\Z]\to\catabgrp$, which is an isomorphism of categories ???. In this way, the notions of torsion, rank, etc, all carry forward.

Since Abelian groups are just $\Z$-modules and $\Z$ is a PID, the Structure Theorem for finitely-generated modules over a PID applies:

Theorem (Structure Theorem for f.g. Abelian Groups). Every f.g. Abelian group $G$ admits a decomposition $G\iso\Z^{\rk G}\oplus\Tor G$, and furthermore $$\begin{equation} \Tor G\iso\bigoplus_{i,j}\Z/\mf{p}_i^{\alpha_{ij}}\iso\bigoplus_j\Z/\mf{a}_j \end{equation}$$ for unique (up to a permutation) prime ideals $\mf{p}_i\nsubgrp\Z$ (elementary divisors) and positive integers $\alpha_{ij}\in\N$, where $\mf{a}_j\coloneqq\prod_i\mf{p}_i^{\alpha_{ij}}$ (invariant factors).

On the other hand, Abelian groups that are not finitely-generated are much wilder, and many set-theoretical issues crop up.
