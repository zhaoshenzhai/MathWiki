Group


The concept of a group was invented to capture the abstract properties of symmetries (i.e. transformations that preserve internal ‘structure’) of geometrical objects. These transformations must be composable in the following coherent way.

Definition.

A group is a tuple $\tpl{G,\cdot,e,\iota}$ consisting of underlying set $G$, a distinguished element $e\in G$ called the identity, and operations $\cdot:G\times G\to G$ and $(\slot)^{-1}:G\to G$, subject to the following conditions.

  1. (Associativity). For all $g,h,k\in G$, we have $g\cdot(h\cdot k)=(g\cdot h)\cdot k$.
  2. (Identity). For all $g\in G$, we have $g\cdot e=g=e\cdot g$.
  3. (Inverse). For all $g\in G$, we have $g\cdot g^{-1}=e=g^{-1}\cdot g$.

A prominent class of examples motivated by the above are isometry groups of metric spaces; more generally, one considers a category $C$, where each object $X\in C$ induces its automorphism group $\Aut X$, with $\cdot$ being interpreted as composition in $C$. In fact, every group arises this way (as the morphisms in the groupoid $\cat{B}G$).

  With group homomorphisms as morphisms, groups form a category $\catgrp$:

Definition. A function $f:G\to H$ between groups is a homomorphism if $$\begin{equation} f(g_1\cdot g_2)=f(g_1)\cdot f(g_2) \end{equation}$$ for every $g_1,g_2\in G$. Note that this implies $f(e_G)=e_H$ and $f(g^{-1})=f(g)^{-1}$.

Perhaps the most important way of studying a group is by analyzing how it acts on a set, or, more generally, on objects in some other category.

Groups, as men, will be known by their actions. $-$ Guillermo Moreno

Very broadly speaking, the goal of group theory is to find classifying invariants for groups, ultimately leading to some kind of Classification of Groups. This is (way) too hard in general; to give some scale, we note that The Hölder Program, consisting of

  1. Classifying all simple groups, which are essentially the ‘atoms’ of group theory;
  2. Classifying all group extensions, which essentially means ‘doing all of chemistry’;

has only been partly solved, with Part 1 taking about $100$ years and $10,000$ pages of hard mathematics to settle, and with Part 2 still relatively open. Nevertheless, many techniques have been developed to study certain classes of groups:

  • Finite groups can be studied combinatorially, and a benchmark problem is to find the number of groups of order $n$. The Sylow Theorems place certain restrictions, but more sophisticated approaches require representation theory.
  • Finitely-generated groups are much more geometric in nature, and are studied mostly in the context of Geometric Group Theory.
  • Abelian groups vary wildly in behavior. Finitely-generated ones are completely classified via the Structure Theorem, but in general they can be very interesting.

One may also equip groups with additional structure, leading to richer theories. Some important examples are topological groups, Lie groups, and algebraic groups.
