Topological Space

Motivation & Definition

Let $X$ be a set. We would like to formalize a notion of ‘closeness’ between two points without necessarily referring to a specific metric, but rather, via certain subsets of $X$ called open sets. The following definition is motivated by some properties $^\dagger$ of open sets generated by a metric $d$ on $X$, which we now axiomatize.

Definition.

Let $X$ be a set. A topology on $X$ is a family $\mc{O}_X\subseteq\pow(X)$, whose elements are called open sets, subject to the following conditions.

  1. (Non-trivial). $\em$ and $X$ are in $\mc{O}_X$.
  2. (Closure under $\cup$). For any $\mc{U}\subseteq\mc{O}_X$, we have $\bigcup\mc{U}\in\mc{O}_X$.
  3. (Closure under finite $\cap$). For any $U_1,U_2\in\mc{O}_X$, we have $U_1\cap U_2\in\mc{O}_X$.

If $\mc{O}_X$ is a topology on $X$, we call the pair $\tpl{X,\mc{O}_X}$ a topological space.

Similarly, continuity of functions between metric spaces $^\dagger$ motivative the following

Definition. A function $f:X\to Y$ between topological spaces is continuous if for any open subset $V\subseteq Y$, its preimage $f^{-1}(V)\subseteq X$ is open too.

  Very broadly speaking, the goal of topology is to find classifying invariants (i.e. a list of properties such that $X\iso Y$ iff they satisfy the same properties), but this is too hard in general. Indeed, the entire field of algebraic topology is devoted to finding algebraic invariants (and techniques to compute them). We thus restrict our attention to certain ‘nice’ subclasses thereof, many of which form subcategories.

  • Hausdorff spaces (and more generally spaces satisfying some Separation axioms), exclude a lot of pathologies present in general topological spaces.
  • Compact spaces exhibit many properties of finite sets, and are therefore really nice to work with.
  • Connectedness and other related concepts like path-connectedness help establish global properties of topological spaces.
  • Separable and $1^\textrm{st}$/ $2^\textrm{nd}$ countable spaces are countable objects (in flavour), giving way to techniques from analysis. This is especially the case for Polish spaces.
  • Sequential spaces allow topological properties (like convergence, continuity, and compactness etc) to be probed by sequences, much like as in metric spaces.
  • Manifolds allow many important properties specific to $\R^n$ to be ‘lifted’ back to the topological space.

One may also equip topological spaces with additional structure(s), leading to richer theories. Some are algebraic in flavor ( topological groups, rings, vector spaces, etc.), some are more geometric ( smooth/ complex manifolds, etc.), and others are both ( Lie groups, ringed spaces, etc.).
