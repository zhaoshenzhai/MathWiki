Topological Space


Let $X$ be a set. We would like to formalize a notion of ‘closeness’ between two points without necessarily referring to a specific metric, but rather, via certain subsets of $X$ called open sets. The following definition is motivated by some properties $^\dagger$ of open sets generated by a metric $d$ on $X$, which we now axiomatize.

Definition.

Let $X$ be a set. A topology on $X$ is a family $\mc{O}\subseteq\pow(X)$, whose elements are called open sets, subject to the following conditions.

  1. (Non-trivial). $\em$ and $X$ are in $\mc{O}$.
  2. (Closure under $\cup$). For any $\mc{U}\subseteq\mc{O}$, we have $\bigcup\mc{U}\in\mc{O}$.
  3. (Closure under finite $\cap$). For any $U_1,U_2\in\mc{O}$, we have $U_1\cap U_2\in\mc{O}$.

If $\mc{O}$ is a topology on $X$, we call the pair $\tpl{X,\mc{O}}$ a topological space.

Similarly, continuity of functions between metric spaces $^\dagger$ motivative the following

Definition. A function $f:X\to Y$ between topological spaces is continuous if for any open subset $V\subseteq Y$, its preimage $f^{-1}(V)\subseteq X$ is open too.

With continuous functions as morphisms, topological spaces form a category, which we denote by $\cattop$. By convention, isomorphisms in $\cattop$ are called homeomorphisms, which are not $^\dagger$ merely bijective continuous functions.

  With the basic terminology established, we now list several branches of topology, each of which shed some light into the structure of $\cattop$.

  • Analysis & Manifolds. Here, we equip topological spaces with extra structure to serve as foundational objects for other fields. Examples include topological vector spaces, manifolds, and metrics, and spaces in these fields are generally Hausdorff, separable, $2^\textrm{nd}$ countable, ( path)- connected, sequential spaces, or even metrizable.
  • Algebraic topology aims to attach algebraic objects to topological spaces that are invariant under homeomorphism, and developing techniques for computing them. This is usually too rigid, however, and most of these objects are in fact homotopy invariant. Examples include the fundamental group and homologies, and spaces in this field are generally locally compact (or even compact) or admit some kind of simplex structure.
